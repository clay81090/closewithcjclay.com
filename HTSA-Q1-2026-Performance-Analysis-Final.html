Executive Summary

This report is not about blame. It's about identifying the specific points in our funnel where revenue is leaking and proposing concrete, sequenced fixes. The data shows a business with genuine product-market fit — $312,500 in February, the single best month in company history — but a funnel with three structurally broken layers that are compounding against each other every week.

Two urgent findings sit above all others. First, the ad engine powering this entire funnel has deteriorated from 289% ROI in January to 44% ROI in March while spend increased 60%. Without intervention, the primary revenue driver approaches break-even within 30–60 days. Second, the team's show rate of 46% — nearly 20 points below industry standard — is the single largest suppressor of otherwise-achievable revenue. Fixing show rate alone, without changing anything else, would add an estimated 10+ closes per month across the team at current close rates.

The business is 3–4 months old as a scaled operation. The ceiling is real and has already been touched. The floor is leaking at three separate points simultaneously. This document identifies exactly where, in exactly what order to fix it.

The Unqualified Lead Problem — A Top-of-Funnel Crisis

The data shows a pattern of applicants reaching the booking stage who have no meaningful understanding of what they are applying for. There is evidence of people who believe they are applying for employment, individuals unfamiliar with what high-ticket sales is, and contacts whose professional backgrounds suggest no plausible path to closing $5,000–$7,000 programs.

This is a funnel design problem, not a marketing problem. When a Facebook ad leads directly to a form that leads directly to a booking calendar with no intermediate qualification, anyone with a smartphone and 60 seconds can consume a closer's calendar slot. The ad creative does its job — it generates interest. The funnel's job is to convert interest into qualified appointments. Right now, it is not doing that job.

The downstream consequences are measurable: unqualified leads consume setter call time, closer calendar slots, follow-up sequences, and confirmation resources — and produce zero revenue. More importantly, high volumes of low-quality clicks train the ad platform's algorithm to find more people like them. A smaller volume of higher-quality leads, generating better click-through and conversion signals, will cause Meta's algorithm to self-optimize toward better prospects over time. Fewer leads at higher quality is a compounding advantage. More leads at low quality is a compounding cost.

◎
Why Marketing Leads Currently Outperform Setter-Booked Leads

Marketing leads — people who book directly through an ad or organic touch — currently show at a 48–52% rate, while setter-booked appointments show at 40–44%. This appears counterintuitive: a setter should be adding qualification and warm-up value that improves show rate. The data shows the opposite.

The reason is intent at the point of booking. A marketing lead who finds the page organically or clicks through from a video they chose to watch has already invested attention before booking. They arrived with some degree of curiosity or pull. A setter cold-calling someone who submitted a form 24–48 hours ago reaches a person who has often cooled down, may not remember the form, and books the appointment to end the call rather than out of genuine interest. When price is disclosed on that setter call — before trust is established — the prospect has hours or days to talk themselves out of it.

The fix is not to reduce setter activity — it's to change the setter's role from mini-closer to qualifier and excitement-builder. A setter call done correctly — short, energetic, price-free, focused on the closer call as the event — should produce show rates above the marketing lead baseline, not below it. The gap between 44% and 52% is entirely recoverable with script and incentive changes.

→
Marketing Fixes — In Priority Order
  • → Maximize the best-performing social videos — redirect, don't reduce them. The concern about friction reducing bookings is valid but misapplied. The solution is not to remove all friction — it's to place the right friction at the right point. Let the social ads run at full volume. Gate the booking step behind a required video watch. Someone who watches 12 minutes before booking has already invested time and attention — they show up. Someone who books in 30 seconds while scrolling will not.
  • → Gate the booking calendar behind the existing VSL. The 27-minute training video already exists. Placing it as a required step before the calendar is shown is a routing change, not a content build. It pre-sells the concept, pre-handles the most common objections, and self-selects for prospects who are genuinely interested. This one change addresses the unqualified lead problem, the no-show problem, and the algorithm quality signal problem simultaneously.
  • → Introduce meaningful application friction — qualifying questions with real answers required. A minimum of 3–4 qualifying questions before any booking access: current income, specific career/financial goal, why now, and at minimum a confirmation that they understand this is a sales training program, not an employment opportunity. People who won't answer four questions will not commit $7,000. Screening them out here protects every downstream resource.
  • → Launch warm retargeting from existing video engagement. People who watched 50%+ of a social video are 3–4x more likely to convert than cold traffic. This audience is being left entirely unused. A warm retargeting sequence using the existing video library is the highest available ROI move and requires no new creative — only audience configuration.
  • → Audit and pause the Google Keyword Only Campaign immediately. $59,354 spent all-time, 30 contacts in Q1 at $1,978 per contact. This is not a search strategy — it is a budget drain. No additional spend should be allocated until a full audit is completed. Meta at $5–8 per contact is the proven channel and should absorb reallocated budget.
  • → Refresh ad creative and expand lookalike audiences before spend is increased. The 289% → 140% → 44% ROI decline is audience saturation and creative fatigue. New creative variations built around the best-performing existing content, combined with fresh lookalike audiences from recent buyers, will recover performance without requiring a new campaign architecture.
  • → Activate email re-engagement to the existing contact database. 26,965 contacts. 7 from email marketing all-time. A segmented re-engagement sequence to the warmest prior contacts — people who submitted a form but never booked, or booked but never showed — represents a significant untapped pool at zero additional ad cost.

✗
The Qualification Bypass — Hidden Cost

The policy of routing leads directly to closers to avoid setter cost is producing the opposite of its intended result. The math is straightforward:

Setter appointment cost: ~$15–30.
Expected revenue value of a qualified closer call slot: ~$1,053 (at 19.5% close rate × $5,400 AOV).
Net cost of bypassing qualification: −$1,023 per wasted slot.

Every unqualified lead that reaches a closer's calendar and no-shows consumes a slot that a qualified prospect could have filled. The $20 setter call is not a cost — it is a $1,000+ investment protection.

✗
The Price Disclosure Problem

Setters are disclosing the $5–7k investment range on the setter call. This creates two cascading problems.

1. Price before trust. The prospect now has 24–72 hours to research alternatives, talk themselves out of it, or find a cheaper option — all before the closer has had a chance to build the emotional commitment that justifies the investment.

2. SDR close rate at 20%. When a setter's booked leads close at 20% — nearly identical to the closer's rate — it means the setter is running a full sales process. This leaves closers with less room, more resistance, and prospects who are already skeptical of a number they've had time to second-guess.

Price belongs to the closer. Full stop.

◎
Recommended Policy: All Leads Through Setter First — With Specific Exception Criteria

The default routing should be: every lead touches a setter before reaching a closer's calendar. No exceptions based on convenience or cost savings. The only permissible exceptions are leads who meet all of the following criteria simultaneously:

  • → Demonstrated existing knowledge of high-ticket sales — not "sales" generally. High-ticket, remote, or commission-based sales specifically. Someone who lists "selling concert tickets" or "retail associate" as their background does not qualify for this exception.
  • → Clear financial urgency and availability — they need to be able to invest within the next 30 days and are not in a financial situation that would make the program inaccessible. This is a qualification call topic, not an assumption.
  • → Completed the VSL or video content before submitting the form — they have demonstrated the minimum engagement level that correlates with show rate. Someone who watched 0 minutes before applying has demonstrated nothing.
  • → Responded to automated follow-up within 2 hours of form submission — active responsiveness is a proxy for genuine interest. Leads who respond immediately are dramatically more likely to show than those chased over days.

If a lead meets all four criteria, a faster direct routing may be appropriate. If they meet zero, one, or two — they go through setter qualification without exception. This is not about being restrictive. It is about protecting closer calendars and the revenue those slots represent.

→
SDR Fixes — In Priority Order
  • → Lead response SOP: first contact within 30 minutes of form submission. Three attempts in the first 2 hours. This is the highest-leverage manual fix available while automation is being built. The 1.8-day average response Q1 average alone accounts for a significant portion of lead death before any setter ever speaks to them. This SOP needs a named owner, a tracking mechanism, and accountability.
  • → Remove price from the setter script — no exceptions. Four jobs only: confirm genuine interest, qualify (urgency, background, financial readiness, why now), build excitement for the closer call specifically, book and confirm. If a prospect asks about price: "That's something [closer name] walks through on the call — it depends on which track makes the most sense for where you are right now." No range. No ballpark. No financing discussion.
  • → Cap setter call at 15–20 minutes. Any setter call exceeding 20 minutes is a setter doing a closer's job. The longer the setter call, the more price-anchoring, objection-handling, and selling occurs — all of which should happen on the closer call with full emotional context established.
  • → Setter owns the confirmation — not the closer. Automated confirmation text + email within 24 hours of booking requiring a confirmed reply. Setter confirmation call 2 hours before every closer appointment. If no response by 1 hour before — cancel the slot and follow up on the team's terms. This responsibility belongs at the setter layer. Placing it on closers fragments focus and duplicates effort.
  • → Two no-show rule — no exceptions. Any prospect who no-shows twice does not receive a third closer appointment. They re-enter setter qualification and must demonstrate renewed engagement before any calendar access is granted. There is currently zero consequence for ghosting. This creates a structural incentive to not show up.
  • → Track setter performance on show rate and close rate — not raw set volume. The highest-value setter is not the one who books the most appointments. It is the one whose appointments show up and close. Chris Vadinsky generates the best conversion outcomes despite not leading in raw volume — this is the model. Compensation and performance evaluation should reflect quality over quantity. A setter whose appointments close at 25% is worth dramatically more than one closing at 10%.
  • → Feed new setters fresh leads from day one. A setter working a November contact list in late March — contacts that are 4–5 months cold — is not being set up to succeed. Fresh lead allocation, defined onboarding territory, and a clear ramp protocol are standard practice and should be in place before a new setter makes their first call.

February Proves The Ceiling — March Proves The Problem Is Upstream

In February, the closing team posted $312,500 — the single best month in company history. CJ Clay completed 115 calls, Will Hemmert 114, Charles Mockus 84, Jason King 44. Revenue grew consistently through the month with no down weeks.

In March, the same four closers, running the same calls, are down 24.8%. Their close rates have not materially changed. Their completed call volume has. Fewer sets (201 → 178 → 161 per month), a declining show rate, and confirmation burdens being carried by closers rather than setters are combining to reduce the number of qualified conversations happening each week.

The closing team cannot close what doesn't show up. The February benchmark is not a ceiling — it is a floor of what this team is capable of when the pipeline delivers qualified, confirmed prospects. The goal is to make February's output the consistent baseline, not the peak exception.

✗
Confirmation Burden Is Misallocated

Closers are currently expected to manage night-before texts, day-of texts, hour-before texts, and personal confirmation calls — in addition to preparing for and executing the actual sales conversation. This is setter work placed on the wrong role.

The setter who booked the appointment has the relationship with the prospect from the initial call. They are the right person to confirm it. When closers carry this burden, it fragments focus, creates duplicate untracked effort, and still doesn't guarantee the slot is protected — because there is no system enforcing it.

Confirmation ownership should be fully transferred to the setter layer with a defined SLA, tracked in HubSpot, and tied to setter performance metrics.

→
What Closers Can Do Right Now (Bridge Measures)

Personal confirmation call — morning of. 60 seconds. "Hey [Name], confirming we're still on at [time] today." Adds 10–15 points to personal show rate while setter confirmation protocol is implemented.

Push same-day or next-day books wherever possible. Temperature drops every day between setter call and closer call. Recency is the most powerful show-rate driver available to closers today.

Pre-call text — one hour before. "Hey [Name], calling you at [time] — reply YES so I know we're set." Simple, low-effort, measurable impact of 5–8 points.

Two no-shows from one contact — stop the slot. Refer back to setter. Protect the calendar from dead weight consuming time that qualified prospects could use.

Structural Issue
Compensation Structure Misalignment
Current incentives reward the wrong behaviors at both layers. The data makes a clear case for realignment.
✗
Current: Volume-Based Setter Pay

Setters are rewarded for the number of appointments booked — regardless of whether those appointments show up, qualify, or close. This is precisely why the data shows setters booking anyone who will agree to a time slot, disclosing price to keep conversations moving, and running long calls that cross into closer territory.

The incentive structure is producing exactly the behavior the data shows. A setter whose appointments don't show is generating cost with no revenue outcome — and the current structure does not penalize this.

→
Proposed: Quality-Based Setter Pay

Base pay per booked appointment (lower than current per-set rate) + performance bonus per closed deal from their booked appointments.

This aligns setter incentive with the outcome that matters: revenue. A setter whose appointments close at 25% earns meaningfully more than one at 10% — as they should. The bonus is self-funding: it is paid from incremental revenue that would not exist without the quality set. Chris Vadinsky's conversion performance is already the model for what this looks like in practice.

✗
Current: RQAPP-Based Closer Pay

Revenue-per-qualified-appointment ties closer compensation to pipeline quality — a variable the closer does not control. When show rates drop due to upstream failures, when unqualified leads consume calendar slots, when confirmation systems fail — RQAPP deteriorates regardless of how well the closer performs on completed calls.

The March data illustrates this exactly: the same closer who produced a company-record February saw RQAPP drop nearly 50% in March — not because close rate changed, but because show rate and pipeline quality changed. Measuring closer performance on variables they do not control is both a retention risk and a measurement error.

→
Proposed: Close Rate-Based Closer Pay

Closer compensation tied to close rate on completed calls — the only variable the closer actually controls. Out of every call that happens, what percentage closes?

This structure protects closers from upstream failures while still holding them accountable for their actual performance. It also makes performance conversations precise: if close rate is declining, it is a skill or fit question. If close rate is stable but revenue is down, the problem is upstream — show rate, set volume, pipeline quality. The two things become separable for the first time.

Automation & Tech Stack
The automation gap is costing revenue every day. A long-term solution is in development. Immediate-deployment options exist right now.
◎
The Current State — And Why It Must Be Resolved This Month

Charlie AI — the planned automation solution for instant lead response, confirmation sequences, and no-show follow-up — is incompatible with the current JustCall phone platform and cannot be deployed. A proprietary AI system is being developed internally as the long-term replacement. This is the right strategic direction: a purpose-built system designed around HTSA's specific funnel will outperform any off-the-shelf tool once completed.

The gap is what happens between now and then. Every day without automated lead response is a day where the 1.8-day average response time remains intact and leads go cold. Every week without confirmation automation is a week where confirmation burden stays on closers. The revenue cost of this gap is measurable and ongoing.

The following solutions can be deployed within days to weeks using existing HubSpot infrastructure, bridging the gap while the proprietary system is completed:

Orum Days to deploy

Parallel dialer — HubSpot native integration. Allows setters to call 3 contacts simultaneously, increasing daily call volume from ~300–400 to 1,000–2,000 per rep. No CRM migration required. Direct HubSpot sync. This is the fastest available fix for setter contact rate and is in active use at comparable organizations.

Koncert Days to deploy

AI parallel dialer with local presence and voicemail drop. HubSpot integration available. Similar 3-line parallel capability to Orum with additional AI features for call routing and disposition logging. Particularly useful for outbound setter campaigns at scale.

Air AI / Bland AI 1–2 weeks to deploy

AI voice agent for instant lead response and follow-up. Calls new form submissions within seconds of submission, qualifies with a scripted conversation, and books directly to setter or closer calendar. HubSpot integration available. Replaces the Charlie AI use case entirely — immediate lead contact, 24/7, with no human required for the first touch.

Salesmsg / Heymarket Days to deploy

SMS automation with HubSpot native integration. Triggers instant text sequences on form submission, books confirmation sequences before closer calls, and manages no-show follow-up automatically. Solves the confirmation SLA problem without any dialer change. Low cost, fast setup, immediate impact on show rate.

GoHighLevel 2–4 weeks to deploy

Full-stack sales and marketing automation platform. Parallel dialing, AI follow-up, SMS/email sequences, calendar integration, and pipeline management in one system. Requires migration from JustCall but eliminates the JustCall integration bottleneck permanently. Used extensively at high-ticket coaching and training organizations.

Proprietary HTSA System Long-term

Custom-built AI system in development. The correct long-term solution — built specifically around HTSA's funnel, scripts, qualification criteria, and CRM architecture. A purpose-built system will outperform any generic tool once deployed. The bridge solutions above are interim measures only, designed to protect revenue while this system is completed.

Recommendation:  Deploy Orum or Koncert for parallel dialing and Salesmsg for SMS confirmation automation this week — both integrate with HubSpot in days. Evaluate Air AI or Bland AI for instant lead response within the next 2 weeks. These do not conflict with the proprietary system build and can be sunset once the custom solution is ready.
